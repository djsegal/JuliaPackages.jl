<p>Solutions to Stokes' differential equation:</p>
<p align="center"><a target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" href="/tex/7a9703279d8af8b6c816345789cfc3d6.svg?invert_in_darkmode&amp;sanitize=true"><img src="/tex/7a9703279d8af8b6c816345789cfc3d6.svg?invert_in_darkmode&amp;sanitize=true" align="middle" width="95.89569494999999pt" height="35.77743345pt/" style="max-width:100%;"></a></p>
<p>From <em>Tables of the Modified Hankel Functions of Order One-Third and of their Derivatives</em>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Its only singularity is an irregular singularity at infinity. The equation occurs in the description of simple cases of diffraction and of refraction of waves.
The general solution of [Stokes' equation] can be written in terms of Bessel functions of order one-third. The tabulation of these Bessel functions for complex arguments would make possible the computation of solutions of [Stokes' equation] for complex arguments. The direct tabulation of solutions of [Stokes' equation] should, however, be preferred to that of Bessel functions of order one-third. Unlike Bessel's equation, <strong>[Stokes' equation] has no singularity in the finite complex plane and its solutions are single-valued</strong>, whereas the Bessel functions of order one-third are not.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>Two solution approaches are used. If <code>abs2(z) &lt; 36</code>, a power series solution is used. Otherwise, an asymptotic expansion is performed because of floating point limits in the power series.</p>

<p>The asymptotic expansions can be used to estimate <a target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" href="/tex/5a95dbebd5e79e850a576db54f501ab8.svg?invert_in_darkmode&amp;sanitize=true"><img src="/tex/5a95dbebd5e79e850a576db54f501ab8.svg?invert_in_darkmode&amp;sanitize=true" align="middle" width="16.02366149999999pt" height="22.831056599999986pt/" style="max-width:100%;"></a>, <a target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" href="/tex/0f7cea0b89929faf20eda59174bc247f.svg?invert_in_darkmode&amp;sanitize=true"><img src="/tex/0f7cea0b89929faf20eda59174bc247f.svg?invert_in_darkmode&amp;sanitize=true" align="middle" width="16.02366149999999pt" height="22.831056599999986pt/" style="max-width:100%;"></a>, and their derivatives, although in general with less accuracy than the power series. Two expansions are required depending on the value of <code>arg z</code>. The existence of two expressions of different forms which represent asymptotically the same integral function is an example of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes_phenomenon" rel="nofollow">Stokes' phenomenon</a>.</p>
