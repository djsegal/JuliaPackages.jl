<h1><a id="user-content-quantumtomographyjl" class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" href="#quantumtomographyjl"><svg class="octicon octicon-link" viewBox="0 0 16 16" version="1.1" width="16" height="16" aria-hidden="true"><path fill-rule="evenodd" d="M4 9h1v1H4c-1.5 0-3-1.69-3-3.5S2.55 3 4 3h4c1.45 0 3 1.69 3 3.5 0 1.41-.91 2.72-2 3.25V8.59c.58-.45 1-1.27 1-2.09C10 5.22 8.98 4 8 4H4c-.98 0-2 1.22-2 2.5S3 9 4 9zm9-3h-1v1h1c1 0 2 1.22 2 2.5S13.98 12 13 12H9c-.98 0-2-1.22-2-2.5 0-.83.42-1.64 1-2.09V6.25c-1.09.53-2 1.84-2 3.25C6 11.31 7.55 13 9 13h4c1.45 0 3-1.69 3-3.5S14.5 6 13 6z"></path></svg></a>QuantumTomography.jl</h1>
<p>This is a package that provides basic routines to perform quantum
tomography of finite dimensional systems.</p>
<p>Given the estimated means and variances of the observations,
tomography proceeds by finding the state, process, or measurements
that optimize some figure of merit -- either the likelihood or the χ²
statistic -- often with the additional constraint the reconstruction
be physical (e.g., correspond to a valid quantum mechanical object).</p>
<p>Namely, states are constrained to be given by positive semidefinite
matrices with unit trace, processes are constrained to be given by the
Choi matrix of a completely positive, trace preserving map, and
measurements are constrained to be given by POVM elements.</p>

<h2><a id="user-content-api" class="anchor" aria-hidden="true" href="#api"><svg class="octicon octicon-link" viewBox="0 0 16 16" version="1.1" width="16" height="16" aria-hidden="true"><path fill-rule="evenodd" d="M4 9h1v1H4c-1.5 0-3-1.69-3-3.5S2.55 3 4 3h4c1.45 0 3 1.69 3 3.5 0 1.41-.91 2.72-2 3.25V8.59c.58-.45 1-1.27 1-2.09C10 5.22 8.98 4 8 4H4c-.98 0-2 1.22-2 2.5S3 9 4 9zm9-3h-1v1h1c1 0 2 1.22 2 2.5S13.98 12 13 12H9c-.98 0-2-1.22-2-2.5 0-.83.42-1.64 1-2.09V6.25c-1.09.53-2 1.84-2 3.25C6 11.31 7.55 13 9 13h4c1.45 0 3-1.69 3-3.5S14.5 6 13 6z"></path></svg></a>API</h2>
<p>Each different tomography method is associated with a type. Objects of
this type must be instantiated before reconstruction can be performed
with a call to <code>fit()</code>. These objects are also needed to make
predictions about tomography experiments with <code>predict()</code>. Currently available
tomography methods are</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><code>FreeLSStateTomo</code>: Unconstrained least-squares state tomography.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><code>LSStateTomo</code>: Least-squares state tomography constrained to yield physical states.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><code>MLStateTomo</code>: Maximum-likelihood state tomography (including the option for entropy maximization or hedging).</p>
</li>
</ul>

<p>In order to perform quantum state tomography, we need an
informationally complete set of measurement effects. In the case of a single
qubit, that can be given by the eigenstates of the 3 Pauli operators.</p>
<div class="highlight highlight-source-julia"><pre>julia<span class="pl-k">&gt;</span> <span class="pl-k">using</span> Cliffords, QuantumTomography

julia<span class="pl-k">&gt;</span> obs <span class="pl-k">=</span> Matrix[ (<span class="pl-c1">complex</span>(<span class="pl-c1">Pauli</span>(i))<span class="pl-k">+</span><span class="pl-c1">eye</span>(<span class="pl-c1">2</span>))<span class="pl-k">/</span><span class="pl-c1">2</span> <span class="pl-k">for</span> i <span class="pl-k">in</span> <span class="pl-c1">1</span><span class="pl-k">:</span><span class="pl-c1">3</span> ];

by using the experimental fitting routine <code>fitA</code> with <code>MLStateTomo</code>:</p>
