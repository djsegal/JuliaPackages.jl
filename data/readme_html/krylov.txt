<p dir="auto">This package provides implementations of certain of the most useful Krylov method for a variety of problems:</p>
<ol dir="auto">
<li>Square or rectangular full-rank systems</li>
</ol>
<p align="center" dir="auto">
  <b><i>Ax = b</i></b>
</p>
<p dir="auto">should be solved when <strong><em>b</em></strong> lies in the range space of <strong><em>A</em></strong>. This situation occurs when</p>
<ul dir="auto">
<li><strong><em>A</em></strong> is square and nonsingular,</li>
<li><strong><em>A</em></strong> is tall and has full column rank and <strong><em>b</em></strong> lies in the range of <strong><em>A</em></strong>.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2" dir="auto">
<li>Linear least-squares problems</li>
</ol>
<p align="center" dir="auto">
  minimize ‖<b><i>b</i></b> - <b><i>Ax</i></b>‖
</p>
<p dir="auto">should be solved when <strong><em>b</em></strong> is not in the range of <strong><em>A</em></strong> (inconsistent systems), regardless of the shape and rank of <strong><em>A</em></strong>. This situation mainly occurs when</p>
<ul dir="auto">
<li><strong><em>A</em></strong> is square and singular,</li>
<li><strong><em>A</em></strong> is tall and thin.</li>
</ul>
<p dir="auto">Underdetermined systems are less common but also occur.</p>
<p dir="auto">If there are infinitely many such <strong><em>x</em></strong> (because <strong><em>A</em></strong> is column rank-deficient), one with minimum norm is identified</p>
<p align="center" dir="auto">
  minimize ‖<b><i>x</i></b>‖   subject to   <b><i>x</i></b> ∈ argmin ‖<b><i>b</i></b> - <b><i>Ax</i></b>‖.
</p>
<ol start="3" dir="auto">
<li>Linear least-norm problems</li>
</ol>
<p align="center" dir="auto">
  minimize ‖<b><i>x</i></b>‖   subject to   <b><i>Ax = b</i></b>
</p>
<p dir="auto">should be solved when <strong><em>A</em></strong> is column rank-deficient but <strong><em>b</em></strong> is in the range of <strong><em>A</em></strong> (consistent systems), regardless of the shape of <strong><em>A</em></strong>.
This situation mainly occurs when</p>
<ul dir="auto">
<li><strong><em>A</em></strong> is square and singular,</li>
<li><strong><em>A</em></strong> is short and wide.</li>
</ul>
<p dir="auto">Overdetermined systems are less common but also occur.</p>
<ol start="4" dir="auto">
<li>Adjoint systems</li>
</ol>
<p align="center" dir="auto">
  <b><i>Ax = b</i></b>   and   <b><i>Aᴴy = c</i></b>
</p>
<p dir="auto">where <strong><em>A</em></strong> can have any shape.</p>
<ol start="5" dir="auto">
<li>Saddle-point and Hermitian quasi-definite systems</li>
</ol>
<p align="center" dir="auto">
  [<b><i>M </i></b>   <b><i> A</i></b>]  [<b><i>x</i></b>]            =           [<b><i>b</i></b>]
  <br>
  [<b><i>Aᴴ</i></b>        <b><i>-N</i></b>]  [<b><i>y</i></b>]    [<b><i>c</i></b>]
</p>
<p dir="auto">where <strong><em>A</em></strong> can have any shape.</p>
<ol start="6" dir="auto">
<li>Generalized saddle-point and non-Hermitian partitioned systems</li>
</ol>
<p align="center" dir="auto">
  [<b><i>M</i></b>   <b><i>A</i></b>]  [<b><i>x</i></b>]            =           [<b><i>b</i></b>]
  <br>
  [<b><i>B</i></b>   <b><i>N</i></b>]  [<b><i>y</i></b>]    [<b><i>c</i></b>]
</p>
<p dir="auto">where <strong><em>A</em></strong> can have any shape and <strong><em>B</em></strong> has the shape of <strong><em>Aᴴ</em></strong>.
<strong><em>A</em></strong>, <strong><em>B</em></strong>, <strong><em>b</em></strong> and <strong><em>c</em></strong> must be all nonzero.</p>
<p dir="auto">Krylov solvers are particularly appropriate in situations where such problems must be solved but a factorization is not possible, either because:</p>
<ul dir="auto">
<li><strong><em>A</em></strong> is not available explicitly,</li>
<li><strong><em>A</em></strong> would be dense or would consume an excessive amount of memory if it were materialized,</li>
<li>factors would consume an excessive amount of memory.</li>
</ul>
<p dir="auto">Iterative methods are recommended in either of the following situations:</p>
<ul dir="auto">
<li>the problem is sufficiently large that a factorization is not feasible or would be slow,</li>
<li>an effective preconditioner is known in cases where the problem has unfavorable spectral structure,</li>
<li>the operator can be represented efficiently as a sparse matrix,</li>
<li>the operator is <em>fast</em>, i.e., can be applied with better complexity than if it were materialized as a matrix. Certain fast operators would materialize as <em>dense</em> matrices.</li>
</ul>

<p dir="auto">All solvers in Krylov.jl have in-place version, are compatible with <strong>GPU</strong> and work in any floating-point data type.</p>
