<p>A hemireal number <code>z</code> can be written</p>
<div class="highlight highlight-source-julia"><pre>z <span class="pl-k">=</span> r <span class="pl-k">+</span> mμ <span class="pl-k">+</span> nν</pre></div>
<p>where <code>r</code>, <code>m</code>, and <code>n</code> are real, and the special numbers <code>μ</code>, <code>ν</code> satisfy</p>
<div class="highlight highlight-source-julia"><pre>μ<span class="pl-k">*</span>μ <span class="pl-k">=</span> ν<span class="pl-k">*</span>ν <span class="pl-k">=</span> <span class="pl-c1">0</span>, μ<span class="pl-k">*</span>ν <span class="pl-k">=</span> ν<span class="pl-k">*</span>μ <span class="pl-k">=</span> <span class="pl-c1">1.</span></pre></div>
<p>Addition, subtraction, and any operation involving real numbers are
defined "the obvious way," and the conjugate of <code>z</code> is just <code>z</code>.
Multiplication of general hemireals is commutative but not
associative.  Hemireals with <code>ν=0</code> are the same as dual numbers.</p>
<p>The motivation for inventing/rediscovering (?) the hemireals was to
solve, using finite numbers, what would otherwise be singular
equations.</p>
