<p dir="auto">Extended-exponent floating-point numbers</p>
<p dir="auto">This package implements "X-numbers" as described by <a href="https://doi.org/10.1007/s00190-011-0519-2" rel="nofollow">Fukushima
(2012)</a> — which refined the ideas of <a href="https://doi.org/10.1145/355934.355940" rel="nofollow">Smith et
al. (1981)</a>.  As Fukushima explained,</p>
<blockquote>
<p dir="auto">...we represent a non-zero arbitrary real number, <code>X</code>, by a pair of an IEEE754 floating point
number, <code>x</code>, and a signed integer, <code>i_X</code>.  More specifically speaking, we choose a certain
large power of 2 as the radix, <code>B</code>, and regard <code>x</code> and <code>i_X</code> as the significand and the
exponent with respect to it.  Namely, we express <code>X</code> as <code>X = x B^{i_X}</code>. The major difference
from Smith et al. (1981) is the choice of the radix...</p>
</blockquote>
<p dir="auto">Note that this does not increase the <em>precision</em> of floating-point operations (the number of digits
in the significand), but vastly increases the range of numbers that can be represented.  Therefore,
X-numbers are not frequently useful in numerical analysis.  Even the standard <code>Float64</code> can
represent numbers of magnitude roughly <code>2^{-1022}</code> to <code>2^{1024}</code>, which is usually sufficient —
whereas the roughly 16 digits of precision can frequently be inadequate.  In such cases, it is
better to use extended-precision arithmetic, provided by packages like <code>DoubleFloats.jl</code>,
<code>Quadmath.jl</code>, <code>ArbNumerics.jl</code>, or the built-in <code>BigFloat</code> type.</p>
<p dir="auto">Instead, X-numbers are useful in very specific cases, like the computation of Associated Legendre
Functions (ALFs) and hence (scalar) spherical harmonics of very high degree.  While the same goals
could be <em>partially</em> achieved using certain other types like <code>BigFloat</code>, X-numbers can be
implemented far more efficiently — requiring anywhere from 10% to a few times longer than similar
computations with the underlying float type.  However, see <a href="https://doi.org/10.1007/s00190-019-01331-0" rel="nofollow">Xing et
al. (2020)</a> for techniques to compute ALFs using
standard float types that can be advantageous in some ways.  X-numbers might also find use in
Machine Learning, where the precision of a <code>Float64</code> is unnecessary, but the range of a <code>Float16</code>
may be too restrictive.</p>
