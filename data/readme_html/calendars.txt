<p dir="auto">The <em>Common Era</em> <code>CE</code> dates are computed according to the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proleptic_Gregorian_calendar" rel="nofollow">proleptic Gregorian</a> rules, <em>Julian dates</em> <code>JD</code> according to the rules of the reformed <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_calendar" rel="nofollow">Roman calendar</a> proposed by Julius Caesar. The <em>European calendar</em> <code>EC</code> uses the Gregorian calendar for dates on and after <code>CE</code> 1582-10-15 and otherwise the Julian calendar.</p>
<p dir="auto">We recommend using the European calendar to avoid errors and confusion by extending the Gregorian calendar rules backward to the dates preceding its official introduction in 1582, as the common calendar <code>CE</code> does. However, if you are not working with dates older than 400 years, this distinction is irrelevant, and the use of <code>CE</code> brings no disadvantages.</p>
<p dir="auto">We do not use the acronym <code>AD</code> ("Anno Domini nostri Jesu Christi") but use <code>JD</code> instead, which stands for the Julian dates. <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anno_Domini" rel="nofollow">AD</a> is an acronym invented by a 6th-century <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysius_Exiguus" rel="nofollow">monk</a>, member of the Roman Curia. Nowadays, the <code>AD</code> terminology is exclusive to non-Christian people. However, we keep the start of the epoch and allow the use of <code>AD</code> as an option.</p>
<p dir="auto">Dates can be converted from one to another. We follow ISO 8601 for calendar date representations: Year, followed by the month, then the day, <code>YYYY-MM-DD</code>. This order is also used in the signature of the functions. For example, 2022-07-12 represents the 12th of July 2022. We extend this notation by prefixing it with two letters, the acronyms of the names of calendars given in the table above: <code>CE-YYYY-MM-DD</code>, <code>JD-YYYY-MM-DD</code>, <code>AM-YYYY-MM-DD</code> and so on.</p>
<p dir="auto">Roughly speaking a calendar date <code>CDate</code> is a pair (CC, YMD) where <code>CC</code> is a calendar specifier and <code>YMD</code> is 3-tuple of integers which, as a string, is written <code>YYYY-MM-DD</code>. However, internally we represent a calendar specifier also as an integer. Therefore a <code>CDate</code> is implemented as 4-tuple of integers extending the calendar date representation of ISO 8601.</p>
<p dir="auto">The primary function provided is:</p>
<div class="highlight highlight-source-julia notranslate position-relative overflow-auto" dir="auto" data-snippet-clipboard-copy-content="ConvertDate(date::CDate, calendar::DPart, string=false::Bool, show=false::Bool)"><pre><span class="pl-c1">ConvertDate</span>(date<span class="pl-k">::</span><span class="pl-c1">CDate</span>, calendar<span class="pl-k">::</span><span class="pl-c1">DPart</span>, string<span class="pl-k">=</span><span class="pl-c1">false</span><span class="pl-k">::</span><span class="pl-c1">Bool</span>, show<span class="pl-k">=</span><span class="pl-c1">false</span><span class="pl-k">::</span><span class="pl-c1">Bool</span>)</pre></div>
<p dir="auto">It converts a calendar date to the representation of the date in the 'calendar.'</p>
<ul dir="auto">
<li>
<p dir="auto">The calendar date <code>CDate</code> is a tuple (calendar, year, month, day). The parts of the date can be given as a tuple or individually.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p dir="auto">'calendar' is one of "Common", "European", "Julian", "Hebrew", "Islamic", or "IsoDate". Alternatively, you can use the acronyms CE, EC, JD, AM, AH, or ID explained in the table above. "Gregorian" is used synonymously with "Common," and AD can be used synonymously with JD.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p dir="auto">If the optional parameter 'string' is set to 'true,' the date is returned as a string in the format <code>CC-YYYY-MM-DD</code>. Otherwise an integer 4-tuple is returned, where the first item is the calendar specifier as defined in the enumeration in the table at the top of this page. By default the integer representation is returned.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p dir="auto">If the optional parameter 'show' is set to 'true,' both dates are printed. 'show' is 'false' by default.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<p dir="auto">But if you look up the birthday of Leonardo da Vinci, you better use the European calendar <code>EC</code> and not the Common calendar <code>CE.</code> This time, the <code>EC</code>-date and the <code>JD</code>-date are the same, and this is correct. But the Gregorian calendar misses Leonardo's birthday by nine days! This error occurs because the Gregorian calendar extrapolates the date backward from 1582, ignoring the historical course. (Or because you forgot that Gregory lived 100 years after Leonardo.)</p>

<p dir="auto">Extrapolating backwards is why the Gregorian calendar is called 'proleptic.' But even that is not correct: <code>proleptic</code> refers to the future; it would be better to call it <code>preleptic.</code> In any case, the message is: use the European calendar, not the Common/Gregorian one.</p>

<p dir="auto">A broader overview give the utility functions <code>PrintEuropeanMonth(year, month)</code> and <code>SaveEuropeanMonth(year, month, directory)</code>. The latter writes the <code>EC-year-month</code> month for all calendar representations to the given directory in a markdown file. The next example is particularly instructive.</p>
<div class="highlight highlight-source-julia notranslate position-relative overflow-auto" dir="auto" data-snippet-clipboard-copy-content="julia&gt; PrintEuropeanMonth(1582, 10)"><pre>julia<span class="pl-k">&gt;</span> <span class="pl-c1">PrintEuropeanMonth</span>(<span class="pl-c1">1582</span>, <span class="pl-c1">10</span>)</pre></div>
<p dir="auto">It shows the month when Pope Gregory severely interfered with Chronos turning the Zodiac wheel: at his behest, Thursday 4 October 1582 was followed by Friday 15 October 1582. To say that the confusion this caused was minor would be the understatement of the millennium. But this break can only be seen in the European calendar; both the Julian and the Gregorian calendars smoothly cross this break as if nothing had happened. (What Gregory's contemporaries saw very differently: they felt cheated of 1/3 of their monthly rent.)</p>

<p dir="auto">According to our convention, the last day represented by the calendar is EC-9999-12-31, has the Julian date number 5373484 and the European date number 3652061. Thus the European calendar gives 3652061 days a name. Both counters differ only by an additive constant. The European date number is formally defined as</p>
<p dir="auto">EN(date) = JN(date) − 1721423.</p>
<p dir="auto">The European date number is similar to the day number <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rata_Die" rel="nofollow">Rata Die</a> defined by <code>R</code>eingold and <code>D</code>ershowitz. In fact, <code>RD</code> differs only by two days from the European date number since <code>RD</code> is based on the proleptic Gregorian calendar and CE-0001-01-01 = EC-0001-01-03. But we think it is much more natural to start at the beginning of the Julian calendar, JD-0001-01-01 = EC-0001-01-01, and not with a 'proleptic' relation.</p>
<p dir="auto">The <code>Julian day number</code> <code>JN</code> is the count of days since the beginning of the Julian Period in 4713 BCE. There is also a <code>continuous</code> form of the Julian day number, <code>JC</code>, containing a fraction that represents a <code>daytime</code>. This form we do not use. Our counts are pure integers, likewise our algorithms do not use real numbers.</p>
<p dir="auto">However, we remark that if a day indicated by a common (midnight based) date starts in the continuous form with the <code>JC</code> N.5 and ends with <code>JC</code> (N+1).4999..., then the corresponding day number <code>JN</code> is (N+1), which could be seen as (N+1).0 in terms of <code>JC</code>, indicating the noon of that day. Conceptually, of course, a counter is a different thing than a point in time.</p>

<p dir="auto">Calendars are more tricky to implement than it first appears. It is therefore essential to compare the results with reference implementations.
